New Products & the Product Life-Cycle

How firms create new products, and how those products rise, peak, and decline.

Core Concepts

DEFINITION

New-Product Development

Developing original products, product improvements, product modifications, and new brands through the firm's own efforts.

WHY FAILURES HAPPEN

Top 5 Reasons New Products Fail

Overestimating market size · design problems · incorrect pricing/positioning · high development costs · competitor reaction.

TWO IDEA SOURCES

Internal vs. External Ideas

Internal: R&D, intrapreneurs, employees. External: customers, competitors, distributors, suppliers, crowdsourcing.

The PLC shows the course of a product's sales and profits over its lifetime. It has five stages: product development → introduction → growth → maturity → decline.

09CHAPTER

Pricing — Capturing Customer Value

The only element of the marketing mix that produces revenue — all others produce costs.

The Three Major Pricing Strategies

FIGURE 9.1 · THE PRICING CORRIDOR
Price Ceiling & Price Floor
STRATEGY 1

Customer Value-Based

Price based on buyers' perceptions of value, not on the seller's cost. Two flavors: good-value pricing (right combo of quality + service at fair price) and value-added pricing (add features/services to differentiate & charge more).

STRATEGY 2

Cost-Based

Setting prices based on cost of producing, distributing, and selling the product plus a fair rate of return. Cost-plus is simplest; break-even / target profit pricing sets price to cover costs and hit a profit target.

STRATEGY 3

Competition-Based

Setting prices based on competitors' strategies, costs, prices, and market offerings. Price must reflect the value delivered relative to competitors.

New-Product Pricing

Market-Skimming

  • Set a high initial price to "skim" revenue from layers of buyers willing to pay more
  • Product quality & image must support higher price
  • Costs of producing smaller volume can't be so high they cancel out the higher price
  • Competitors shouldn't be able to enter easily
  • Example: Apple iPhone launches

Market-Penetration

  • Set a low initial price to attract a large number of buyers quickly & win big market share
  • Market must be highly price-sensitive
  • Production & distribution costs must fall as sales volume increases
  • Low price must help keep out competition
  • Example: Walmart, IKEA

Product-Mix Pricing · 5 Types

TYPE 1
Product Line
CLICK TO FLIP
Setting price steps between product line items based on cost differences, customer evaluations, and competitor prices. Example: Rolex sells Oyster Perpetual up through Cellini Prince tiers.
TYPE 2
Optional-Product
CLICK TO FLIP
Pricing of optional or accessory products sold alongside the main product. Example: car sold with options like leather seats, sunroof, premium sound system.
TYPE 3
Captive-Product
CLICK TO FLIP
Pricing products that must be used with a main product. Example: razors (cheap) + blades (expensive); printers + ink cartridges; game consoles + games.
TYPE 4
By-Product
CLICK TO FLIP
Setting a price for by-products to make the main product's price more competitive. Example: zoos selling composted animal waste.
TYPE 5
Product Bundle
CLICK TO FLIP
Combining several products & offering the bundle at a reduced price. Example: fast-food combo meals, cable + internet + phone bundles.

Price Adjustment Strategies · 7 Forms

ADJUSTMENT 1

Discount & Allowance

Cash discounts, quantity discounts, functional (trade) discounts, seasonal discounts, trade-in allowances, promotional allowances.

ADJUSTMENT 2

Segmented Pricing

Selling at two+ prices, where difference is not based on costs: customer-segment, product-form, location, time pricing.

ADJUSTMENT 3

Psychological Pricing

Price says something about the product — e.g., $9.99 feels meaningfully cheaper than $10.00; high prices signal quality.

ADJUSTMENT 4

Promotional Pricing

Discounts, special-event pricing, cash rebates, low-interest financing, longer warranties, free maintenance.

ADJUSTMENT 5

Geographical Pricing

FOB-origin · uniform-delivered · zone · basing-point · freight-absorption pricing.

ADJUSTMENT 6

Dynamic Pricing

Adjusting prices continually to meet needs of individual customers & situations — especially prevalent online (e.g., Priceline, Uber surge).

ADJUSTMENT 7

International Pricing

Price depends on economic conditions, competition, laws, regulations — plus channels & buyer perceptions in each country.

10CHAPTER

Marketing Channels & Value Delivery

Getting the product from producer to the final consumer.

KEY TERM

Marketing Channel

A set of interdependent organizations that help make a product or service available for use/consumption by the consumer or business user.

KEY TERM

Value Delivery Network

The company + suppliers + distributors + customers who partner to improve the performance of the entire system.

KEY TERM

Supply Chain

Upstream: firms supplying raw materials/components. Downstream: channel partners linking firm to customer.

Channel Levels — Direct vs. Indirect

Channel Functions

Transaction Completion

Information

Gathering & distributing marketing research & intelligence.

Promotion

Developing & spreading persuasive communications.

Contact

Finding & communicating with prospective buyers.

Matching

Shaping offers to buyer's needs (manufacturing, grading, assembling, packaging).

Negotiation

Reaching agreement on price & terms so ownership can transfer.

Transaction Fulfillment

Physical Distribution

Transporting & storing goods.

Financing

Acquiring & using funds to cover channel work costs.

Risk Taking

Assuming the risks of carrying out the channel work.

Channel Behavior & Organization

CONFLICT TYPES

Horizontal vs. Vertical Conflict

Horizontal: conflict among firms at the same level (e.g., two Ford dealers competing). Vertical: conflict between different levels of the same channel (e.g., Ford vs. its dealers).

SYSTEM · VMS

Vertical Marketing System

Producer, wholesaler & retailer act as a unified system. Three types: corporate (one ownership), contractual (franchise), administered (power of one large member).

SYSTEM · HMS

Horizontal Marketing System

Two+ companies at one level joining together to pursue a new marketing opportunity (e.g., McDonald's inside Walmart stores).

SYSTEM · MULTI

Multichannel Distribution

A single firm sets up two+ channels to reach one or more customer segments.

Distribution Intensity — Pick One

INTENSITY 1
Intensive
CLICK TO FLIP
Stocking the product in as many outlets as possible. Used for convenience goods — candy, toothpaste, Coca-Cola. Goal: maximum availability.
INTENSITY 2
Exclusive
CLICK TO FLIP
Giving a limited number of dealers exclusive rights to distribute in their territories. Used for luxury & specialty goods — Rolex, Lamborghini. Goal: strong image, high dealer support.
INTENSITY 3
Selective
CLICK TO FLIP
Use more than one but fewer than all of the intermediaries willing to carry the product. Used for shopping goods — TVs, furniture, appliances.
11CHAPTER

Retailing & Wholesaling

The last-mile players who bring products to final buyers.

DEFINITION

Retailing

All the activities involved in selling goods or services directly to final consumers for their personal, non-business use.

DEFINITION

Wholesaling

All activities involved in selling goods & services to those who buy for resale or business use.

EMERGING

Shopper Marketing

Using point-of-sale promotions & ads to extend brand equity to "the last mile" — encouraging favorable in-store purchase decisions.

Types of Retailers — 4 Classification Axes

Retailers are classified by (1) amount of service, (2) product line, (3) relative prices, and (4) organizational approach.

By Service Level

Self-Service

Customers do their own locate-compare-select. Lowest cost. Ex: discount stores, warehouse stores.

Limited-Service

More sales assistance; carry shopping goods requiring info. Ex: Sears, JCPenney.

Full-Service

Usually carry more specialty goods requiring assistance/advice. Highest cost. Ex: high-end specialty & department stores.

By Product Line

NARROW + DEEP

Specialty Store

Narrow product line, deep assortment within that line. Example: Athleta, Foot Locker.

WIDE VARIETY

Department Store

Wide variety of product lines, each operated as a separate department. Example: Macy's, Nordstrom.

LOW-MARGIN HIGH-VOLUME

Supermarket

Large, low-cost, low-margin, high-volume, self-service store carrying wide variety of grocery & household products.

SMALL + LONG HOURS

Convenience Store

Small, near residential areas, open long hours 7 days a week, limited line of high-turnover goods. Example: 7-Eleven.

MEGA-GROCERY

Superstore

Much larger than regular supermarket; offers large assortment of routinely purchased food + non-food + services.

DEEP SPECIALTY

Category Killer

Giant specialty store carrying very deep assortment of a particular line. Example: Best Buy, Home Depot.

SERVICE

Service Retailer

Product line is actually a service: hotels, airlines, banks, colleges, hospitals.

By Relative Prices

LOWER PRICES

Discount Store

Sells standard merchandise at lower prices/margins in return for higher volume. Example: Walmart, Target.

BELOW-WHOLESALE

Off-Price Retailer

Buys at less-than-regular wholesale prices and sells at less than retail. Includes factory outlets, independents, warehouse clubs (Costco).

By Organizational Approach

CHAIN · COMMON OWN

Corporate Chain

Two or more outlets commonly owned and controlled. Example: Gap, Starbucks.

CHAIN · INDEP

Voluntary Chain

Wholesaler-sponsored group of independent retailers engaged in group buying & common merchandising.

COOP

Retailer Co-op

Group of independent retailers banding together to set up a jointly owned wholesale operation.

CONTRACT

Franchise

Contractual association between franchisor & independent franchisees who buy the right to own & operate units. Example: McDonald's, Subway.

Advertising & Public Relations

Communicating customer value to geographically dispersed audiences.

CORE DEFINITION

Promotion Mix

The specific blend of promotion tools a company uses to persuasively communicate customer value & build customer relationships.

The 5 Major Promotion Tools

THE PROMOTION MIX
Five tools that communicate customer value
TOOL 1

Advertising

Paid form of nonpersonal presentation & promotion of ideas, goods, or services by an identified sponsor. Reaches geographically dispersed buyers at low cost per exposure.

TOOL 2

Sales Promotion

Short-term incentives to encourage the purchase/sale of a product or service. Strong but short-lived effects.

TOOL 3

Personal Selling

Personal presentation by the firm's sales force to make sales & build customer relationships. Most effective — but most expensive — tool.

TOOL 4

Public Relations (PR)

Building good relations with various publics via favorable publicity, building up a good corporate image, and handling unfavorable events. Very believable.

TOOL 5

Direct Marketing

Direct connections with carefully targeted individual consumers to obtain an immediate response and cultivate lasting customer relationships.

Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC)

DEFINITION

IMC

Carefully integrating and coordinating the company's many communications channels to deliver a clear, consistent, and compelling message about the organization & its products. Recognize all customer contact points; deliver a consistent positive message at each.

Advertising Objectives — The 3 Purposes

NEW CATEGORY

Informative

Heavy at introduction of a new-product category. Objective: build primary demand.

COMPETITIVE

Persuasive

Important as competition increases. Objective: build selective demand. Some becomes comparative / attack advertising.

STAY TOP-OF-MIND

Reminder

Important for mature products. Maintains customer relationships & keeps consumers thinking about the product.

Sales Promotion — Definition & Objectives

DEFINITION

Sales Promotion

Short-term incentives to encourage purchase or sale of a product/service. Used with other promotion mix tools to reinforce product position & build long-term relationships.

RAPID GROWTH

Why It's Surged

Product managers face pressure to increase sales · competitors use it to differentiate · advertising efficiency has declined · more thrift-oriented consumers.

Three Target Types of Sales Promotion

!

① Consumer Promotion Tools

FREE TRIAL

Samples

Offers of a trial amount of a product.

DISCOUNT CERT

Coupons

Certificates that save buyers money on specified products.

POST-PURCHASE

Cash Refunds / Rebates

Price reduction after purchase — manufacturer refunds part of purchase price.

SHELF PRICING

Price Packs / Cents-Off

Savings off the regular price of a product offered directly on the package.

BONUS ITEM

Premiums

Goods offered free or at low cost as an incentive to buy a product.

BRANDED ITEMS

Advertising Specialties

Useful articles imprinted with an advertiser's name/logo (pens, mugs, T-shirts).

IN-STORE

POP Promotions

Point-of-purchase displays and demonstrations at the point of sale.

CHANCE-BASED

Contests, Sweepstakes, Games

Give consumers the chance to win something — cash, trips, goods.

EXPERIENTIAL

Event Marketing

Creating a brand-marketing event, or serving as sole/participating sponsor of events created by others.

② Trade Promotion Tools

Used to persuade resellers to carry a brand, give it shelf space, promote it, and push it to consumers.

Contests · premiums · displays · discounts / price-off / off-invoice / off-list · allowances · free goods · push money (a.k.a. "spiffs" — cash payments to dealers' salespeople to push the product) · specialty advertising items.

③ Business Promotion Tools

Used to generate business leads, stimulate purchases, reward customers, and motivate salespeople.

SHOW FLOOR

Conventions & Trade Shows

Vendors find new leads, contact customers, introduce products, sell more & educate customers.

INTERNAL

Sales Contests

For salespeople or dealers to motivate them to increase sales performance over a given period.

14CHAPTER

Direct & Online Marketing

Building direct, one-to-one relationships with carefully targeted consumers.

CORE DEFINITION

Direct Marketing

Connecting directly with carefully targeted segments or individual consumers, often on a one-to-one, interactive basis. For many companies (GEICO, Amazon), direct marketing is a complete model for doing business.

Benefits — Buyers vs. Sellers

To Buyers

  • Convenient & easy
  • Private
  • Immediate & interactive
  • Access to wealth of products
  • Comparative info on companies, products & competitors

To Sellers

  • Builds customer relationships
  • Low-cost, efficient, speedy alternative to reach markets
  • Online → lower costs, improved efficiency
  • Greater flexibility
  • Access to buyers unreachable through other channels

Customer Database — The Foundation

KEY CONCEPT

Customer Database

An organized collection of comprehensive data about individual customers or prospects — including geographic, demographic, psychographic, and behavioral data. Fuels personalized, customer-triggered promotional messages.

Forms of Direct Marketing

POSTAL

Direct-Mail Marketing

Sending an offer, announcement, reminder, or other item directly to a person at a particular address. Allows high target selectivity, can be personalized, flexible, easy to measure.

PRINT / DIGITAL

Catalog Marketing

Marketing through print, video, or digital catalogs. Online catalogs: unlimited merchandise, broader presentation formats, real-time merchandising.

VOICE

Telemarketing

Outbound: sell directly to consumers/businesses. Inbound: toll-free 800 numbers receive orders from TV/print ads, direct mail, or catalogs.

TV

Direct-Response TV (DRTV)

Direct-response TV advertising (infomercials) and interactive TV (iTV) advertising.

PHYSICAL · AUTO

Kiosk Marketing

Product/service information & ordering machines placed in stores, airports, hotels, campuses. Example: Redbox DVD rental kiosks.

WEB

Online Marketing

Efforts to market products/services and build customer relationships over the Internet.

Online Marketing Players

ONLINE ONLY

Click-Only Companies

"Dot-coms" that operate online only, with no brick-and-mortar presence. Include e-tailers, search engines, portals, transaction sites, content sites, online social networks. Example: Amazon (historically), Google.

HYBRID

Click-&-Mortar Companies

Traditional brick-and-mortar companies that have added online marketing to their operations. Example: Staples, Walmart, Target.
